The analysis established that this market is a plain, constant-parameter Gaussian simulator with one market factor, two sector factors, and exactly one primary exploitable signal: cross-instrument lead-lag, where instrument i's move today carries information about instrument j's move tomorrow. Momentum, trend, drift, own-reversal, volatility clustering and any nonlinear structure were each tested and found absent. A second, weaker and orthogonal signal — cross-sectional residual reversal — earns a minority weight.

The strategy rebuilds the lead-lag matrix daily on market-neutral residuals, drops entries within one standard error as noise, aggregates the surviving structure into a prediction for every instrument, blends in the reversal sleeve at its information-coefficient-optimal weight, applies a hysteresis band so it does not pay commission to flip on a marginal signal, and then takes the maximum permitted position in every instrument, neutralising net market exposure with ALGO.

The number to trust Parameters were chosen using only windows scored on days 125–350, and every sharp optimum was re-confirmed on three independent evaluation grids. On the holdout (days 350–500), never used for any decision, the strategy scores a mean of 463.6, a minimum of 371.7, and is positive on every window. A model whose lead-lag matrix is frozen on days 1–250 and traded blind on 251–500, never refit, still scores 355.7 at Sharpe 3.73. That last figure is the most conservative honest estimate of how the strategy travels to data it has never seen.
The checks that matter most A placebo using identical machinery but a random signal loses $234 per day. A second placebo, with a random lead-lag matrix but the real reversal sleeve, loses $33 per day. If there were a look-ahead bug, both would profit. Neither does. Together they prove the edge is real, and that the lead-lag matrix — not the reversal sleeve — is carrying the strategy.

We had the best Sharpe on the board. That was the problem. frac = sr²/(sr²+1) is saturated above Sharpe ≈ 5. We kept 97.8% of our profit; the leader kept 96.5%. Everything in version 2 follows from that single realisation: score ≈ mu. That changes the sizing problem from a mean-variance optimisation into a linear programme, and it makes turnover — which costs real dollars — matter far more than volatility, which costs almost none.

The game

What eval.py actually rewards

We implement getMyPosition(prcSoFar). Each day it receives price history shaped (51 instruments, days-so-far) and returns 51 integer share positions. The scorer replays the last 250 days, hands us only the past, clips our positions to the dollar limit at that day's price, charges commission on shares traded, and records daily profit and loss. The score is:

$$\mathrm{sr} = \frac{\sqrt{250}\,\mu}{\sigma}, \qquad \mathrm{frac} = \frac{\mathrm{sr}^2}{\mathrm{sr}^2 + 1}, \qquad \text{score} = \begin{cases} \mu \cdot \mathrm{frac} & \mu > 0\\[2pt] \mu & \mu \le 0\end{cases}$$

Read it as dollar profit, taxed by unreliability. You keep 50% of profit at Sharpe 1, 80% at Sharpe 2, 90% at Sharpe 3, and the curve then flattens. Two consequences drive every design decision that follows.

Asymmetry A profitable strategy can never score negative; volatility only shrinks the score toward zero. But a losing strategy scores its full loss with no discount. Hence: signal quality first, size second.
Size is nearly free once Sharpe is high Doubling every position doubles $\mu$ and doubles $\sigma$, so $\mathrm{sr}$ and $\mathrm{frac}$ are unchanged and the score simply doubles. Score is linear in size until the caps bind. So: reach a high Sharpe, then deploy capital to the limits.

Where the $\sqrt{250}$ comes from

Variance of a sum of independent days adds, so a year's variance is $250\times$ a day's and its standard deviation is $\sqrt{250}\times$ a day's. But the mean grows by the full $250$. So $\mathrm{SR}_{\text{year}} = \sqrt{250}\,\mathrm{SR}_{\text{day}}$. Mean grows linearly, risk only as a square root. That gap is why a tiny daily edge, repeated, becomes a large annual Sharpe. It is the entire economic basis of this strategy.

Method

The one principle everything hangs on

We are scored on a hidden window of days we have never seen. A number measured on our 500 days is worthless unless it reflects a stable property of the generator rather than a fluke of this sample.

We cannot see the future, so we simulate the situation. Split the data in half. Treat the first half as "data we have" and the second as "the unseen future". Estimate the same quantity separately on each half, then correlate the two sets of per-instrument values across instruments:

$$\text{stability}(\theta) = \operatorname{corr}_{i=1..N}\!\left(\theta_i^{(1)},\, \theta_i^{(2)}\right)$$

Near $1$ means the instrument that scored high early scores high late: a real, persistent fingerprint we may build on. Near $0$ means the first half tells you nothing about the second: sample noise, to be discarded however good it looks in a backtest. This is the stability audit, and it is the single filter that decides what enters the strategy.

The three numbers that are the whole strategy Volatility stability 0.982 (trust it, size on it) · Beta stability 0.780 (usable, but noisy — shrink it) · Drift stability −0.034 (momentum is dead, never bet on it).

Part I · 2

Prices are the wrong unit; log returns are the right one

A "+1" move is $+0.9\%$ on a 116 stock and $+6.7\%$ on a 15 stock. Price levels are not comparable, so they cannot be averaged or correlated. Returns fix that. We use log returns for two reasons.

They add across time $100\to110\to121$ is two $+10\%$ days. Simple returns give $0.10+0.10=0.20$, but the true move is $0.21$; you must compound. Log returns give $\ln(1.1)+\ln(1.1)=\ln(1.21)$ exactly, because $\ln\frac{P_1}{P_0}+\ln\frac{P_2}{P_1}=\ln\frac{P_2}{P_0}$. Every statistical tool we use is built for sums.
They are symmetric $+10\%$ then $-10\%$ simple leaves you at 99. In log space $+x$ then $-x$ is exactly zero, since $\ln(P/Q)=-\ln(Q/P)$. Needed before assuming a symmetric distribution.

We lose nothing: $\ln(1.01)=0.00995$ against $r=0.01$. The notebook verifies the transform is lossless by asserting $P_0\,e^{\sum_t g_t} = P_T$.

Part I · 3

The distribution question rules whole toolkits in or out

A Gaussian is fully described by mean and variance, and for jointly Gaussian variables all dependence is linear, captured entirely by correlation. So if returns are Gaussian, linear tools capture everything there is, and machine learning — whose job is finding nonlinear structure — has nothing to find. It would memorise flukes in 499 observations and fail on the unseen days.

Kurtosis: the fat-tail meter

$$\operatorname{kurt}(x)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\tfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^{4}\right]$$

The fourth power is dominated by extremes: a $4\sigma$ day contributes $256$, a $1\sigma$ day contributes $1$. A Gaussian gives exactly $3$, which follows from $\phi'(z)=-z\phi(z)$ and one integration by parts: $\mathbb{E}[Z^4]=3\,\mathbb{E}[Z^2]=3$. Hence excess kurtosis $=\operatorname{kurt}-3$, so $0$ means Gaussian tails. Skew is the same construction with the third (odd) power, so it keeps sign and detects a longer tail on one side.

Finding Returns are Gaussian, symmetric and thin-tailed. Forbidden on evidence: tail hedging, crash machinery, and deep or nonlinear ML. Licensed: linear statistics, which are provably sufficient.
Part I · 4

Volatility: no clustering, wide dispersion, and rock-solid stability

Being Gaussian overall says nothing about ordering. In real markets big moves cluster. To isolate magnitude we square the returns, which throws away sign; and since daily means are $\approx0$, $\operatorname{Var}(r)\approx\mathbb{E}[r^2]$, so each $r_t^2$ is that day's contribution to variance. The question "does volatility cluster?" becomes "is $r_t^2$ autocorrelated?"

Lag1235102-SE band
Mean autocorr of $r^2$−0.006−0.004+0.001+0.003~0±0.090

Zero at every lag, on $r^2$ and on the more robust $|r|$. Real markets show $\approx+0.2$ at lag 1. A GARCH(1,1) models $\sigma_t^2=\omega+\alpha r_{t-1}^2+\beta\sigma_{t-1}^2$; with no clustering, $\alpha,\beta\to0$ and it collapses to a single constant variance. So GARCH and volatility timing are off the table, and each instrument's risk is one fixed number.

How big a number? Annual volatility spans 15.7% to 64.7%, a 4.1× spread. Equal-dollar sizing would let the wild names dominate risk entirely. And the calmest instrument is ALGO — the second clue that it is a diversified average. Then the stability audit:

Volatility split-half stability = 0.982 About as stable as an empirical parameter ever gets. The ranking of instruments by riskiness barely moves between halves. Our risk unit is validated: measure it on data we hold, rely on it for data we do not.
Part I · 5

ALGO is the market

Average pairwise correlation across all 2,550 off-diagonal entries is +0.200. Under independence each pairwise correlation would scatter around zero with $\operatorname{SE}\approx 1/\sqrt{T}=0.045$, and their average would be essentially zero. A systematic $+0.200$ is the footprint of a shared factor.

To identify it: an index is the average of its constituents, because averaging 50 instruments cancels their independent idiosyncratic wiggles and leaves only the common movement. So we test ALGO against the equal-weight basket of the other 50 on three things that each say something different.

Correlation and slope are not the same thing Both divide the same covariance, but by different things: $\rho = \frac{\operatorname{cov}}{\sigma_x\sigma_y}$ while $\text{slope}=\frac{\operatorname{cov}}{\sigma_x^2}$, hence $\text{slope}=\rho\cdot\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x}$. They coincide only when the volatilities match, which here they happen to. Had ALGO's volatility been double, the slope would read $\approx2$ with an unchanged correlation. Part I · 6

Exactly three factors, and the phantom fourth

The correlation matrix $C$ is a machine: feed it any portfolio of weights $w$ and it returns that portfolio's variance, $w^\top C w$. Some portfolios swing hard ("hold everything": the common factor reinforces), others barely swing ("long one, short another": it cancels). Maximising $w^\top C w$ subject to $\lVert w\rVert=1$ gives, via a Lagrange multiplier, $C w = \lambda w$. So the special directions are the eigenvectors, and

$$\operatorname{Var}(w) = w^\top C w = w^\top(\lambda w) = \lambda.$$

The eigenvalue is the variance of the portfolio described by its eigenvector. This is PCA, derived rather than named. Because we use the correlation matrix, every instrument is standardised to variance 1, so the eigenvalues must sum to $N=51$ (the notebook asserts this). In a world with no common factor the variance spreads evenly and every eigenvalue is $\approx1$.

Why "$\lambda>1$" is not good enough

With only 499 days, unrelated instruments show sample correlations of $\pm0.045$ by luck, and across 1,275 pairs those accidents conspire. Even with no structure the eigenvalues spread around 1. Marchenko-Pastur gives the exact ceiling luck can reach, with $q=N/T$:

$$\lambda_{\max}=\bigl(1+\sqrt{q}\bigr)^2 = 1.74$$
An assumption we violate, and the correction Marchenko-Pastur describes a null world with no structure. Ours has a market factor absorbing 22.7% of the variance, so the remaining factors compete for less variance than the theorem assumes and the honest ceiling is lower. We compute the market-adjusted ceiling $\tilde\lambda_{\max}=\left(1-\frac{\lambda_1}{N}\right)\bigl(1+\sqrt{q}\bigr)^2 = 1.35$ and accept the conclusion only if it survives the stricter bar. It does.

An independent cross-check

If every pair had the same correlation $\rho$, then $C=(1-\rho)I+\rho\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ and exactly $\lambda_1 = 1+(N-1)\rho$. Plugging in the average pairwise correlation from a completely different calculation: $1 + 50\times0.200 = 10.98$, against an observed $11.55$. Agreement within 4.9%. Two unrelated calculations agreeing means neither is a coding error, and the small excess is the sector factors leaking extra co-movement into the first component.

Part I · 7

Beta, residuals, and the collapse that makes everything work

Beta is not a definition; it is derived. Choose $b$ to leave the smallest unexplained residual:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial b}\sum_t\bigl(r_{i,t}-b\,r_{m,t}\bigr)^2=0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \beta_i=\frac{\operatorname{cov}(r_i,r_m)}{\operatorname{Var}(r_m)}$$

Setting $i=m$ gives $\beta_m=1$ exactly — a free correctness check the notebook asserts. Betas average 0.98 (range 0.50 to 1.68), as they must, since the market is the average of the instruments.

Beta split-half stability0.780Persistent and usable, but noticeably noisier than volatility's 0.982, because beta is a ratio of two estimates and inherits noise from both. Shrink it.

Why $-0.010$ and not exactly zero?

Not a bug. ALGO is almost exactly the cross-sectional average, so subtracting $\beta_i r_{m,t}$ approximately demeans each day across instruments. Demeaned numbers must sum to zero, so if 49 are above average the 50th is forced below: a mechanical negative correlation of exactly $-\frac{1}{N-1}=-0.0204$ for perfect demeaning. The notebook verifies this by demeaning exactly (giving $-0.0169$) and confirms our hedge lands part-way there, as it should for an approximate demeaning.

The consequence that makes the strategy simple After the hedge the 50 bets are near-independent, so the residual covariance matrix is approximately diagonal. The mean-variance optimal position, which in general requires inverting a $50\times50$ covariance matrix estimated from 499 days (a statistical nightmare), therefore collapses to $\text{dollars}_j \propto \text{prediction}_j / \sigma_j$. Inverse-volatility sizing is not a heuristic here. It is the optimal solution, given that the hedge made the bets independent.

Part II · 9

Lead-lag: the only surviving signal

$$L_{ij}=\operatorname{corr}\!\left(z_{i,t},\,z_{j,t+1}\right)=\frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{t}z_{i,t}\,z_{j,t+1}$$

Built on market-neutral residuals, so we are not merely rediscovering that everything follows the market. The diagonal is own-autocorrelation, already dead, so it is zeroed. That leaves $50\times49=2{,}450$ cross relationships. We are about to run 2,450 statistical tests, so we defend three ways rather than one.

Defence 1: generate the null instead of assuming it

The textbook says $2450\times2(1-\Phi(3))\approx6.6$ entries should exceed three standard errors by chance — but that assumes the entries behave like independent Normal draws, which is an assumption about our data. So we generate chance: permute the day ordering 300 times, which destroys any $t\to t+1$ relationship while preserving each day's cross-sectional structure, and recount.

Part II · 10

Why a stability of 0.110 is still worth a fortune

A split-half correlation of $0.110$ looks hopeless next to volatility's $0.982$. Two honest answers, and it is easy to tell a comforting story here, so we tested one and it failed.

What 0.110 means. Correlating two independent noisy measurements of the same truth gives the fraction of measured variance that is real signal. So roughly $11\%$ of each matrix entry is structure and $89\%$ is estimation noise. The entries are mostly noise. That is a fact, not spin.

A tempting story that we tested and refuted One might hope that summing over 50 instruments makes the prediction far more stable than the individual entries, because noise cancels. We measured it directly: entry stability $+0.110$ versus prediction stability $+0.096$. They are the same. Aggregation does not stabilise the predictor. Any claim that it does would be wishful thinking, and an earlier draft of this project made exactly that claim before the test refuted it.

The real reason a weak signal pays: breadth. What matters is not the stability of one entry, but how much predictive information the aggregate carries, applied across many bets. Grinold's fundamental law:

$$\mathrm{IR}\;\approx\;\mathrm{IC}\times\sqrt{\text{breadth}}$$

We measure an out-of-sample information coefficient of $\mathrm{IC}=0.048$ (matrix fitted on the first half, scored on the second, $t\approx5.0$, positive on 61% of days). With 50 instruments traded on each of 250 days, breadth is at most $12{,}500$, so $\mathrm{IR}\lesssim 0.048\times\sqrt{12{,}500}\approx 5.4$.

Our realised Sharpe of 4.97 sits just under that theoretical bound, which is exactly what a correctly-implemented aggregation of this signal should produce. A per-day IC of 0.048 is unremarkable; the same 0.048 applied to 12,500 bets a year is not.

Caveats, since the law is an idealisation: the bets are not fully independent, the formula ignores commission, position limits and the score's Sharpe tax, and it assumes a stable IC. It bounds what is possible; it does not promise it. Only the backtest settles that.

Part II · 11

Cross-sectional reversal: real, orthogonal, and weaker

Instruments that outperformed their peers over the past 20 days tend to underperform next: $s_{i,t}=-\sum_{u=t-19}^{t}e_{i,u}$. We score it with the information coefficient, the cross-sectional correlation between today's signal and tomorrow's residual return.

Two traps defused. Overlapping windows: consecutive signals share 19 of 20 days, so the daily ICs are not independent and the naive $t$ overstates. A Newey-West correction with Bartlett weights barely moves it ($2.66\to2.58$), because the IC series' own autocorrelation is only $0.082$. A single number hides instability: so we split it.

Part III · 12

The backtester: earn the right to make claims

Nothing in Part I or II touched the score function. Predictive information is not profit once position limits, commission and the Sharpe tax apply. So before writing a single position we built backtester.py, a replica of eval.py's calcPL, generalised to score any strategy on many windows and return a score distribution rather than one lucky point.

Four subtleties that are easy to get wrong

  • Commission is charged into cash with a one-day lag (the comm subtracted on day $t$ is day $t-1$'s).
  • The first test-day iteration is a warm-up that sets position but is not scored.
  • Positions are re-clipped to integer share limits at each day's price, even if you do not trade.
  • The P&L standard deviation is population (ddof=0), not sample.
Correctness gate passed Run on the starter strategy, the backtester reproduces eval.py to the cent: $\mu = 10.3597$, $\sigma = 1640.1991$, dollar volume $5{,}919{,}621$, score $0.1023$. eval.py prints $10.4$, $1640.20$, $5{,}919{,}621$, $0.10$. Identical. Only now may we trust its verdicts on our own strategy.
Part III · 13

The anti-overfitting protocol

The easiest way to produce a beautiful, worthless backtest is to tune parameters on the window you then report. So we split the days by role, not just by time.

SetScored daysUsed for
Selection[125, 350)Every parameter choice. Six overlapping 100-day windows.
Holdout[350, 500)Nothing, until the configuration was frozen. Three windows.

Each score is additionally out-of-sample in time, because the strategy only ever sees prices up to the current day and refits daily. And we ran four independent falsification tests on the final configuration:

  1. Holdout. Score on windows never used to choose anything.
  2. Frozen model. Estimate the matrix once on days 1–250 and trade 251–500 blind, never refit. This is the strictest possible test of whether the structure is stationary.
  3. Placebo. Identical machinery, but a random matrix of matched scale. If there is a look-ahead bug it will profit. It must lose.
  4. Plateau, not peak. Prefer a parameter sitting on a flat region over one at a sharp maximum, and re-check any peak under several different evaluation grids.
Pre-registration The configuration was written down and justified before the holdout was run once. The holdout number below is therefore a genuine test, not another tuning knob.
Part III · 14

Strategy design: every choice traces to a measured fact

Each day, causally from history only:

# 1. log returns
r = np.diff(np.log(prcSoFar), axis=1)

# 2. market-neutral residuals. Beta is FULLY shrunk to 1.0, so we just subtract ALGO.
resid = r - r[0]
R = resid[1:]                               # alpha universe: instruments 1..50

# 3. standardise, then build the lead-lag matrix
Z = (R - R.mean(1, keepdims=True)) / sd[:, None]
L = Z[:, :-1] @ Z[:, 1:].T / n
np.fill_diagonal(L, 0.0)                    # own-autocorrelation is dead
L[np.abs(L) < 1.0 / np.sqrt(n)] = 0.0       # drop sub-one-s.e. entries as noise

# 4. aggregate into a prediction, make it dollar-neutral
sig = L.T @ Z[:, -1];  sig -= sig.mean()

# 5. size: mean-variance optimal collapses to prediction / volatility
dollars = sig / sd
dollars *= (1.2 * 50 * 0.95 * 10_000) / np.abs(dollars).sum()
dollars = np.clip(dollars, -9500, 9500)

# 6. hedge net market exposure with ALGO (beta = 1 for all)
hedge = np.clip(-dollars.sum(), -95_000, 95_000)

Version 2 · design

The four changes, in order of value

1 · Hysteresis band — the largest single gain

The daily prediction is noisy. Flipping a position's sign on a marginal signal pays commission for nothing and whipsaws you out of positions that were about to work. So: keep yesterday's sign unless the signal is convincing, specifically unless |sig| > 0.30 × mean|sig|.

A broad hump on all three grids. We chose 0.30 by the pre-registered rule: among configurations within 2% of the best mean, take the one with the best worst window. Dollar volume fell from $129M to $103M, but the gain is far larger than the ~$10/day of commission saved. The band lets winners run, which is the interesting part — see the caveats.

2 · Bang-bang sizing

Because score ≈ mu, the sizing problem is: maximise mu = Σ dᵢ·E[rᵢ] subject to |dᵢ| ≤ cap. That is a linear programme, and its solution is bang-bang:

$$d_i \;=\; \text{cap}\cdot\operatorname{sign}(\text{prediction}_i)$$
This does not contradict the analysis Section 7 proved that inverse-volatility sizing is mean-variance optimal, given that the ALGO hedge makes the residual covariance near-diagonal. That remains true. But mean-variance optimality answers a different question from "maximise dollar profit under a position cap". When the Sharpe tax is saturated, the second question is the one the competition asks. Under bang-bang, volatility and any conviction exponent drop out entirely: only the sign of the prediction matters.

A side benefit: the bang-bang book carries real net exposure, so the ALGO hedge grows from a mean of $17.8k to as much as $99.6k. ALGO's 10× cap, previously 82% idle, is finally doing work.

3 · The exact cap — this was a bug in v1

Version 1 set TARGET_FRAC = 0.95 "to leave headroom, because eval.py re-clips daily at that day's price". That reasoning was wrong. Read the scorer:

curPrices = prcHistSoFar[:, -1]          # the SAME last price our function sizes on
posLimits = (dlrPosLimit / curPrices).astype(int)
newPos    = np.clip(newPosOrig, -posLimits, posLimits)

There is no price drift between our decision and the clip. Verified: 0 involuntary trims in 12,750 checks at 99.9% of cap. Taking exactly floor(CAP / price) shares recovered 5% of the book for free, lifting the eval-window score from 354.90 to 373.16.

4 · The reversal sleeve, at its theoretically optimal weight

For two orthogonal signals, the optimal blend weights each by its information coefficient. The analysis measured lead-lag at IC 0.0484 and reversal at 0.0166 out of sample, with a cross-signal correlation of −0.008 (orthogonal). So:

$$w_{\text{rev}} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{IC}_{\text{rev}}}{\mathrm{IC}_{\text{ll}}+\mathrm{IC}_{\text{rev}}} \;=\; \frac{0.0166}{0.0484+0.0166} \;=\; 0.257$$

The empirical plateau on the selection set runs 0.25 to 0.30. Theory and measurement agree, which is precisely what distinguishes a principled parameter from a fitted one. We use 0.25. Note that in v1's sizing scheme this sleeve looked like a mean-versus-worst-case trade-off and we rejected it; under the corrected sizing it strictly dominates, improving the mean, the worst window and the Sharpe simultaneously.

General round

Upcoming: the one-shot honest test

Everything measured so far — the stability audits, the selection/holdout split, the frozen model, the placebos — is our best attempt to simulate being scored on data we have never seen. But every one of those numbers is still drawn from the same 500-day sample and the same generator seed. The 16 July data release is the first time we get to find out for real.

The discipline that matters most Score the frozen v2 configuration on the new data exactly once, and write the number down, before changing a single parameter. That single number is the honest out-of-sample test this entire project has been building toward. If you re-tune first, you consume it and can never get it back.

Checklist when the data lands

  1. mkdir general_round; drop in the organisers' new prices.txt and eval.py.
  2. Copy strategies/v2_leadlag_reversal_bangbang.py in as general_round/too_much_alpha.py.
  3. Run cd general_round && python eval.py once. Record the score, mean P&L and standard deviation.
  4. Compare against the conservative expectation from the frozen test (355.7) rather than the eval-window figure (480.65).
  5. Seed general_round/analysis.ipynb from the test-round notebook and re-run it. The structural findings should hold if the generator is unchanged: Gaussian returns, no volatility clustering, three factors, volatility stability ≈ 0.98, momentum dead. If any of those break, the generator changed and the strategy must be rebuilt, not tuned.
  6. Only then iterate, using the same selection/holdout protocol on the new days. Record the result as strategies/v3_*.py.

What to attack first, if the frozen number holds

  1. Understand the hysteresis gain. It lifted mean P&L by $103/day while saving only ~$10/day of commission. The likely cause is multi-day persistence in the combined signal. Fit L_k[i,j] = corr(z_i[t], z_j[t+k]) for k = 1…5 and look at the decay. If the signal really does persist, an explicit multi-day-horizon model should beat a band, and this may be worth more than everything in v2.
  2. Replace the band with a transaction-cost-aware optimisation: trade toward the target only when the expected gain exceeds the commission.
  3. Test dropping the hedge. Under bang-bang, the ALGO hedge reaches $99.6k. Hedging reduces volatility, but volatility is nearly free while the Sharpe tax is saturated. The commission saved is real.
  4. Investigate the regressed worst window (selection min fell 250.3 → 242.8 from v1 to v2 even as the mean and holdout improved).

Honesty

Caveats, weakest claim first

1 · Everything is measured on days 1–500, which we hold The selection/holdout split, the frozen test and the placebo are the strongest defences available, but both halves still come from the same 500-day sample and the same generator. The only truly honest test is the next data release. Validate on it; do not re-tune on it until validated. Of all our numbers, the frozen result (242.8) is the one to quote as an expectation, not the 345.6.
2 · The reversal variant beat us on the holdout, and we did not switch ALT (+reversal, weight 0.2) scored 369.7 mean / 308.2 min on the holdout, against PRIMARY's 353.9 / 277.7. It won on both metrics. We did not switch, because choosing it after seeing the holdout would consume the holdout and invalidate the very number that makes it look good. This is exactly the discipline that separates a real result from a flattering one. The right way to settle it is to score both on the July 16 data drop, once, and then pick.
3 · The one-standard-error threshold is a hump, not a plateau Scores fall from 293 at $\text{signif\_se}=1.0$ to 248 at 1.2 and 194 at 1.4. We defended it by confirming the same ordering under three different evaluation grids and by noting that 1.0 s.e. is a canonical, principled cut rather than an arbitrary optimum. It is still the least flat parameter in the strategy and the most likely to disappoint.
4 · Gross scaling deliberately exploits the position caps We scale gross exposure past the caps and clip, which puts most positions near $9,500. If the hidden window has materially different price levels, the daily re-clip could force involuntary, commission-charged trims. The 0.95 target-fraction is the headroom against this, but it is calibrated on prices we have seen.
5 · The breadth argument is an idealisation $\mathrm{IR}\approx\mathrm{IC}\sqrt{\text{breadth}}$ assumes independent bets, ignores commission, position limits and the Sharpe tax, and assumes a stable IC. Our 50 residual bets are near-independent (pairwise correlation −0.010) but not exactly so. The law bounds what is possible; the backtest, not the law, is the evidence.

Next steps, in order of expected value

  1. July 16 drop: score PRIMARY and ALT once each, without re-tuning. Pick the winner. This simultaneously validates the whole methodology and settles the reversal question.
  2. Shrink the matrix rather than threshold it. We tested a hard one-s.e. cut and a hard SVD truncation. A smooth James-Stein style shrinkage, $L \to L \cdot \frac{\text{signal var}}{\text{total var}}$ entry by entry, is the statistically principled middle ground we did not try.
  3. Multi-lag structure. We only tested lag 1. If $i$ leads $j$ by two days, we are leaving that on the table.
  4. Cost model for the clip. Quantify how much score the daily re-clip actually costs, and whether a slightly lower target fraction pays for itself.
